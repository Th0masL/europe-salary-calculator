How it works. For each location the data gives total employer cost and net pay at a few gross-salary points; the tool interpolates linearly between them and inverts the relationship, so you can fix the employer budget, the gross, or the target net and solve for the rest.

Four data sources (switch with the Data toggle): Consensus (default) — per location and metric, the median of the sources after discarding any value >10% from the median of the others; the most-aligned, outlier-resistant estimate. Employer cost also folds in Rippling (a cost-only source, accurate where the others drift). 2025 eBook — Boundless' “Understanding Employment Costs in Europe in 2025”, a fixed snapshot at €32k/€60k/€150k. Skuad — the engine behind Boundless' online calculator. Deel — Deel's employee-cost and take-home calculators (its EOR management fee removed from the cost). The three use different methodologies and can disagree — comparing them is the point. Skuad and Deel are live snapshots, fetched on the date shown under the headline. (Two other calculators were evaluated and not used: Remote.com — employer cost wildly off for ~14 countries; and Papaya Global — its data is stale, from tax years 2021-2023.)

US cities (🇺🇸) are state-level (Seattle→WA, San Francisco→CA, New York→NY, Austin→TX, Atlanta→GA, Miami→FL). Consensus uses a direct statutory calculation — 2025 federal brackets, FICA (with the Social Security cap), and state tax incl. NYC local tax — with no EOR vendor involved. It's validated against Deel's independent per-state figures (agree to <1% everywhere except NYC, which differs only because we include the local tax Deel omits). Deel shows its own per-state calc. 2025 eBook and Skuad fall back to a single-benchmark blog estimate (one USD point, flat rate), marked ≈ and rougher. Employer cost for the US is small (~8–10% on top of gross — mostly FICA) versus ~50%+ in much of Europe. Cost of living stays city-specific.

Figures are estimates for comparison only — they ignore personal allowances, marital status, regional taxes, benefits and bonuses, and currency fluctuations. Do not use them as precise payroll or budgeting figures. “After living costs” subtracts the estimated annual cost of living for a single person (capital city for countries, the city itself for US entries).
